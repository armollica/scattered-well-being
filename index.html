How do I read the graphic?

This is a data-distorted map of Mexico. Each square is a different. municipality. Bigger squares are municipalities with larger populations.

There are six well-being indexes: income, education, housing, health, jobs and safety. The higher the index the better. The darker the color, the higher the index. In the case of the income index, a municipality whose residents have high incomes will be colored dark blue and those with low incomes will be colored light blue.

You can see the data sources and how the well-being indexes were calculated here: Well-Being Index Calculation

How can I interact with the graphic?

Focus on a municipality:

Move your cursor over a square to see the information on a specific municipality. The bar chart shows how the municipality ranks in relation to all other municipalities. When a bar goes to the left it means the municipality is "below average" and when it goes to the right it is "above average". (the center is actually the median, not the mean). If a municipality has a well-being index that is greater than 90% of all other municipalities there will be a plus sign (+) at the end. Similarly, a minus sign (-) indicates the municipality has an index lower than 90% of all others.

Change the well-being index:

To change the well-being index that's displayed, click on the corresponding index on the legend to the right. The graphic starts out with the income index displayed.

Filter the municipalities:

Move your cursor over the different colors on the legend will limit the municipalities displayed to those that are in the hovered index and quantile. For example, if you hover over the dark grey box on the far right of the safety row, you will only see municipalities with high safety indexes.

Case studies:

Look in depth at the well-being measures for specific regions of Mexico.

Mexico City

The capital city (officially known as the Distrito Federal) is the most densily populated area in Mexico. Mexico City ranks highly in most of the well-being measures. If you move you mouse over the darkest color for each map, you can see that most of the municipalities in Mexico City rank in the 80th percentile.

There is one exception — safety. Most municipalities in Mexico City are below average — often in the 40th percentile or lower.

Guadalajara

The capital of Jalisco, Guadalajara and its surrounding metro area comprise the second largest city of Mexico. Like Mexico City Guadalajara ranks highly in most well-being measures. Unlike Mexico City, however, Guadalajara ranks more favorably in the safety ranking. Most of the municipalities are in the 40th-60th percentile.

The entire state of Jalisco ranks quite highly in education, even outside of Guadalajara. If you cursor over many of the smaller municipalities to the south of Guadalajara you can see that many rank in the 90th percentile or better.

The Border Cities

The border cities of Tijuana and Juárez (the two largest squares below) are among the highest ranking municipalities in terms of income and jobs. This is likely due in part to their close proximity to the United States.

The trade-off for any potential job-seekers planning to move to the area is safety. Both municipalities rank among the lowest in terms of safety due to the high rates of murder and sexual offense in the area.

The Safe South

Despite ranking relatively poorly in terms of income and education, the southern states of Oaxaca, Tlaxcala and Puebla are among to safest according to this measure of safety.

The Education Divide

Looking at these maps you get the sense that many of the differences in the well-being indexes can be explained by whether an area is urban or rural.

Here appears to be one exception. In terms of education there is a clear divide along the border between the high ranking areas of Jalisco (left side of map) and the low ranking area of Michoacán de Ocampo (middle of map).

Looking at the jobs index, we see no similar division along state boundaries.
